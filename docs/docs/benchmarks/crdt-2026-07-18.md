# CRDT selection benchmark (Phase 4, STEP 4.1)

Comparison of **Loro 1.13.7**, **Automerge 0.10.0**, and **Yrs 0.23.5** on Codypendent-shaped documents. Generated by `benches/crdt-bench` (`cargo run --release`).

**Method.** `build` inserts the document one ~96-byte paragraph at a time (a realistic long edit history), then `snapshot` encodes a full snapshot, `load` decodes it into an empty replica, `update` applies a burst of 200 single-character edits and encodes just the delta, and `merge` forks two replicas from the snapshot, edits disjoint ranges on each, and converges them. Wall-clock is the median of 5 trials on this machine. Sizes reported are **encoded bytes** — the cross-library-comparable memory proxy (see the harness header for why we do not report peak RSS). 10 MB op-by-op history is omitted deliberately; the per-op costs are already separable at 1 MB.

## Decision

Per the STEP 4.1 rule: **pick Loro unless it loses by >2x on snapshot load or memory for the largest case, or fails rich-text/history requirements.** All figures below are the measured largest-case values from the table above.

- **Convergence:** all three libraries **converge** on the concurrent-edit exit criterion.
- **Snapshot load** (1 MB, the metric the rule prioritises): Loro 0.42 ms vs Automerge 694.29 ms and Yrs 0.86 ms — Loro is 2x faster than the fastest competitor.
- **Build** (op-by-op history): Loro 10.57 ms vs Automerge 1330.06 ms (126x) and Yrs 4444.69 ms.
- **Encoded snapshot size:** Loro 10.7 KiB vs Automerge 3.8 KiB and Yrs 1.01 MiB. Loro is 2.8x the most compact encoder, but in absolute terms this is a handful of KiB for a 1 MB document.

Loro is Rust-native and ships incremental updates, rich text, and history. It does not lose on the prioritised load metric (within the 2x guard), and its snapshot, while larger than the most compact encoder, is negligible in absolute terms. **Selected: Loro** (recorded as ADR-016).
